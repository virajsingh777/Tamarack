Data Center Political Climate

A practitioner's guide for hyperscaler-targeted land plays — energy, tax, zoning, and permitting risk

Washington State

Legislative & Regulatory Landscape

The Core Story

Washington is in a regulatory interregnum. The legislature tried and failed to pass sweeping data center regulation in 2026 — but the underlying pressure hasn't gone away. Expect the 2027 session to produce something more aggressive. In the meantime, the UTC (Utilities and Transportation Commission) and Governor's Data Center Workgroup are filling the void administratively. If you're acquiring land for a site flip, the regulatory uncertainty itself is a pricing variable you need to model.

HB 2515 — "Addressing Emerging Large Energy Use Facilities" Dead — 2026 Session
The flagship bill that would have imposed the most far-reaching data center regulations in state history. Defined any facility using >20 MW as an "emerging large energy use facility." Would have required utilities to file dedicated tariffs ensuring data centers bear their own infrastructure costs, imposed 80% clean energy procurement by 2031 and 100% by 2046, mandated interruptible-load protocols, and barred cost-shifting to residential ratepayers. Passed the House 51–41 but died in the Senate. The framework it proposed is almost certainly the template for 2027 legislation.
Source: Davis Wright Tremaine, Mar. 20, 2026 — dwt.com  |  OPB, Feb. 12, 2026 — opb.org
SB 6231 — Sales Tax Exemption Rollback Enacted — Effective July 1, 2026
Signed by Gov. Bob Ferguson. Eliminates the sales tax exemption for data center operators who replace or refurbish existing server equipment. The exemption for new equipment in qualifying new data centers remains. Prior to this, operators paid zero on the 6.5% state sales tax for server refreshes — typically needed every 3–5 years. This materially changes the economics of existing WA facilities and reduces the competitive advantage for brownfield flips. New greenfield builds in eligible counties are still exempt on initial equipment.
Source: E&E News / Politico, Apr. 8, 2026 — eenews.net  |  Leyton, Apr. 27, 2026 — leyton.com
WA Utilities & Transportation Commission — Large Load Policy Docket Active — Decision Expected Late 2026
After HB 2515 died, the UTC launched its own multi-month rulemaking to establish policy for large-load connections (>20 MW) to investor-owned utilities. The commission will issue a policy statement covering interconnection requirements, cost allocation, and ratepayer protections for facilities like PacifiCorp and Puget Sound Energy customers. This directly affects which sites are viable and what interconnection costs look like. Consumer-owned utilities (Grant PUD, Chelan PUD, Douglas PUD) are not covered by this docket — which is relevant because Eastern WA hydro-rich counties use co-ops, not investor-owned utilities.
Source: KHQ / Spokane News, Apr. 21, 2026 — khq.com
Executive Order 25-05 — Data Center Workgroup Active — Final Report Expected 2026
Gov. Ferguson signed this days after taking office. Convened a cross-agency workgroup (Dept. of Revenue, Commerce, UTC, Ecology, utilities, labor, environmental advocates). Preliminary report delivered Dec. 1, 2025: top recommendation was that Washington's Clean Energy Transformation Act (CETA) and Climate Commitment Act cover any fossil or unspecified power used by data centers. Final report expected later this year and will directly frame 2027 legislation. The workgroup explicitly flagged that WA lacks property tax incentives that 37 other states offer — a fact that could go either way in future sessions.
Source: WA Data Center Workgroup Preliminary Report, Dec. 1, 2025 — dor.wa.gov (PDF)
RCW 82.08.986 — Existing Sales Tax Exemption (Remaining) In Force (Partial)
New data centers with qualifying server equipment and power infrastructure still receive the sales tax exemption in most counties. Urban counties (population over 800K) are capped at 6 certificates per calendar year — a binding constraint in King and Snohomish counties. Rural counties (under 100 persons/sq mi, or under 225 sq mi) are unlimited. Requires minimum investment of $200M and 35+ new jobs to qualify. Eastern WA counties (Grant, Douglas, Kittitas) remain the clearest path to uncapped exemptions. Note: a Spokane County-specific exemption bill was introduced in Feb. 2026 but its fate is unclear.
Source: WA Dept. of Revenue — dor.wa.gov  |  Spokane news, Feb. 9, 2026 — spokesman.com
SEPA — State Environmental Policy Act Baseline Requirement
Every WA project requires environmental review under SEPA (WAC 197-11). For most data centers, the lead agency is the local jurisdiction. Additionally, any new water withdrawals require a separate water right permit from the Department of Ecology. Air permits are required if the project will have backup diesel generators (a near-universal data center feature) — these are regulated by local clean air agencies or Ecology depending on county. WA's Coastal Zone Management Program applies to the 15 western coastal counties. For projects near tribal lands, government-to-government consultation is required and can affect timelines substantially.
Source: WA Dept. of Ecology — ecology.wa.gov
→ Acquisition Implication for WA

Washington is the riskier of the two states for near-term land plays. The regulatory framework is unsettled, not absent — and that means your hyperscaler counterparties will want lower-risk contractual terms on land they're buying. Rural eastern WA (Grant, Douglas, Kittitas counties) remains the most defensible position: uncapped tax exemptions, cheap hydro, and consumer-owned utility coverage that sidesteps the UTC docket. The urban pipeline around Seattle is constrained by the 6-certificate cap and likely will tighten further. Underwrite assuming the HB 2515 framework passes in 2027 — that means any site over 20 MW should model mandatory clean energy procurement from day one.

Oregon

Legislative & Regulatory Landscape

The Core Story

Oregon moved faster than Washington — and its rules are now live, not proposed. The POWER Act is enacted and being implemented right now. Oregon is not hostile to data centers — it currently has plans to quadruple the available development acreage — but the era of cheap cross-subsidized power is over. Oregon also has harder land use constraints than WA, particularly the Goal 14 urban growth boundary rules.

HB 3546 — POWER Act (Protecting Oregonians With Energy Responsibility) Enacted June 2025 — Implementation Ongoing
First-in-the-nation law of its type. Creates a standalone large-load rate class for data centers and cryptocurrency operations at investor-owned utilities (Portland General Electric, Pacific Power, Idaho Power). Applies to facilities using >20 MW. Key provisions now being enforced: (1) data centers pay full lifetime costs of any infrastructure built for their demand; (2) renewable energy requirements must be met before coming online; (3) exit fees if a project is abandoned mid-construction; (4) a surcharge for facilities over >100 MW to fund energy efficiency upgrades for low-income customers (1 cent per kWh). PGE implemented its new rate structure as of June 10, 2026. Pacific Power's proceeding is expected to conclude November 2026. Does not apply to consumer-owned utilities (EWEB, PPL).
Source: OPB, May 12, 2026 — opb.org  |  Oregon Environmental Council, May 12, 2026 — oeconline.org  |  Climate Solutions, May 23, 2026 — climatesolutions.org
Oregon PUC Order — PGE Rate Structure (Effective June 10, 2026) Enacted — May 5, 2026 PUC Order
The specific implementing order from the Oregon PUC: (1) PGE data center customers previously paid ~8¢/kWh vs. residential customers paying ~20¢/kWh — that gap is being closed; (2) data centers must comply with PGE's greenhouse gas emissions obligations under HB 2021; (3) annual reporting on electricity usage is mandatory; (4) data centers must continue paying for infrastructure for its full useful life, even if the data center closes. Columbia Riverkeeper described the PUC agreeing with its arguments "on nearly every issue." Pacific Power's equivalent proceeding is still underway with a November 2026 decision expected.
Source: Columbia Riverkeeper, June 2026 — columbiariverkeeper.org  |  IDCNOVA, May 20, 2026 — idcnova.com
HB 2021 — Clean Electricity and Coal Transition Plan Existing Law — Applies to Data Centers
Oregon requires 50% renewable electricity by 2040 and 100% clean electricity by 2040 for large utilities (PGE, Pacific Power). The PUC's POWER Act order explicitly requires data centers in PGE's territory not to undermine this obligation. For site selection, this means any large data center coming online in PGE territory must factor in renewable procurement — it's not optional compliance.
Source: US Data Map — usdatamap.com  |  National Law Review, Dec. 8, 2025 — natlawreview.com
Statewide Land Use Goal 14 — Urban Growth Boundary Requirements Ongoing Constraint
Oregon's Goal 14 land use planning law is one of the tightest in the nation. Data centers must generally be sited within urban growth boundaries (UGBs) or in designated industrial zones. This limits greenfield options significantly in rural areas without first going through a UGB expansion process, which is slow and politically contested. Key markets: Washington County (Hillsboro) is maxed out on industrial-zoned land in many areas; The Dalles (Wasco County) has available industrial land and has been accommodating to Google; Morrow and Umatilla counties in eastern Oregon offer rural industrial land with existing power infrastructure.
Source: US Data Map — usdatamap.com  |  Oregon CUB — oregoncub.org
DEQ Tier 4 Streamlined Data Center Air Permit Available Program
Oregon DEQ created a streamlined permitting pathway specifically for data centers that use EPA Tier 4 certified backup diesel generators (the cleanest diesel standard). As of 2025, Oregon had over 40 data centers permitted or proposed with 6,000 MW of backup generating capacity — double the statewide residential usage. Facilities that commit to Tier 4 engines can access faster air permitting. This is relevant to your buyers: hyperscalers increasingly require this in their specifications, and having Tier 4 compatibility pre-established in your site analysis is a selling point.
Source: Oregon DEQ — oregon.gov/deq
Property Tax Incentives — Local Enterprise Zone Program In Force
Oregon allows local governments to grant unlimited property tax exemptions with no state cap and no mandatory job creation requirements. In 2026, data centers statewide avoided more than $450 million in Oregon property taxes. This is a significant competitive advantage over Washington, which has no property tax incentive program. The Oregonian has reported that schools lost $275M from these exemptions in 2024, which is creating political pressure — but no legislation eliminating these incentives has advanced. Combined with no state sales tax, Oregon's upfront cost advantage for new builds is substantial. Watch for political blowback in 2027.
Source: Oregon CUB, May 2026 — oregoncub.org
ORS 308.519 — State Assessment Threshold Statutory Requirement
Oregon data center real and tangible personal property is subject to state-level central assessment (rather than county assessment) when the historical cost of Oregon data center property reaches $200 million or more. Below that threshold, property is assessed locally. This distinction matters for how large hyperscaler campuses are taxed vs. smaller single-tenant facilities you might flip.
Source: ORS 308.519 — oregon.public.law
→ Acquisition Implication for OR

Oregon is a more stable environment than Washington right now — the regulatory framework is set, not speculative. The POWER Act is a known cost that hyperscalers can model. The bigger risk for land plays is the Goal 14 constraint: if you're looking at rural land outside UGBs, you're likely buying a long, expensive entitlement fight. Eastern Oregon (Morrow, Umatilla, Wasco counties) is the clearest opportunity — industrial zoning exists, power infrastructure is in place, and consumer-owned utilities like OTEC don't fall under the POWER Act rate mandates. Hillsboro/Washington County is land-constrained. Oregon's no-sales-tax + unlimited property tax exemption combination still makes it compelling for hyperscalers, but that window could narrow in 2027 given school funding pressure.

Federal

Federal Overlay — What Overrides State Rules, What Doesn't

FERC — Six Large Load Interconnection Orders Issued June 18, 2026
On June 18, 2026, FERC unanimously approved six orders requiring all major U.S. grid operators to fast-track AI data center grid connections within 30- and 60-day federal deadlines, while explicitly barring utilities from shifting interconnection costs to existing ratepayers. This applies to PJM, MISO, SPP, CAISO, and others — the Pacific Northwest falls under its own BPA/WECC jurisdiction, so the direct applicability to WA/OR varies. But the federal direction of travel is clear: DC wants data centers built faster and is applying pressure at every level. This does not override state land use, zoning, or retail utility regulations.
Source: TechTimes, June 20, 2026 — techtimes.com  |  hdata.com, May 5, 2026 — blog.hdata.com
Trump Executive Order (July 2025) + "Ratepayer Protection Pledge" (March 4, 2026) Federal Policy
The Trump administration has made rapid data center construction a national priority, streamlining federal environmental review for facilities over 100 MW or $500M investment. However, per MultiState (May 2026), these executive orders "don't override state authority on land use, zoning, or utility regulations." States with independent air quality programs have maintained their own review timelines. The Ratepayer Protection Pledge is voluntary — major hyperscalers agreed to cover electricity production costs — and does not bind state regulators in WA or OR.
Source: MultiState, May 1, 2026 — multistate.us
→ Bottom Line on Federal vs. State

Federal tailwind is real but partial. Washington and Oregon retain full authority over land use, zoning, retail utility rates, and state environmental review. The federal push speeds up transmission interconnection studies but doesn't shortcut county entitlements, SEPA/Oregon EIS processes, or the POWER Act rate structure. Don't let a buyer use "federal support" as a reason to skip state-level due diligence on your sites.
