Washington State

Legislative & Regulatory Landscape

The Core Story

Since our last update, Washington has seen an acceleration of municipal-level resistance that the state legislature could not deliver. Seattle and Spokane both passed one-year data center moratoria in June 2026, and Skagit County passed a six-month moratorium earlier in the month. The state regulatory framework remains unsettled — the UTC docket is active, the workgroup report is due, and the 2027 legislative session looms — but the community opposition factor has now become a concrete, binding constraint on western Washington site selection, not just a political risk to monitor.

Municipal Moratoria — New June 2026

Active Moratoria in Washington State
Seattle
1-year ban on new data centers over 20 MVA, with 6-month extension option. Passed 9–0. Covers all permitting, acceptance, and approval. Triggered by 5 proposed hyperscale projects totaling 369 MW from Seattle City Light's ~1 GW grid. Two companies pulled back; Equinix and Prologis still considering.
Enacted Jun 9, 2026
Spokane
1-year ban on new large data centers, passed 6–1 (Ordinance C36887). Triggered by Avista utility disclosing a nonbinding agreement with an unnamed customer seeking 500 MW — more than half the combined power of all Spokane County customers. Avista has paused those negotiations. Public hearing set July 22, 2026.
Enacted Jun 22, 2026
Skagit County
6-month moratorium passed earlier in June 2026 by the board of commissioners.
Jun 2026

Note: Moratoria apply within city/county limits only. A project sited outside Spokane city limits in unincorporated Spokane County — such as the former Kaiser Aluminum smelter site in Mead, which sits adjacent to BPA's Bell Substation — would not be directly blocked by the city moratorium, though city water service could be withheld.

HB 2515 — "Addressing Emerging Large Energy Use Facilities" Dead — 2026 Session
The flagship bill that would have imposed sweeping data center regulations statewide. Defined any facility using >20 MW as an "emerging large energy use facility." Would have required utilities to file dedicated tariffs, imposed 80% clean energy procurement by 2031 and 100% by 2046, mandated interruptible-load protocols, and barred cost-shifting to residential ratepayers. Passed the House 51–41 but died in the Senate. The Data Center Coalition has since publicly argued the bill could have been salvaged with better "operational conditions" — suggesting negotiations may resume. The Data Center Workgroup's final report and the UTC's large-load docket are both expected to feed directly into a 2027 revival of this framework.
Source: Davis Wright Tremaine, Mar. 20, 2026 — dwt.com  |  The Center Square, Jun. 23, 2026 — thecentersquare.com
SB 6231 — Sales Tax Exemption Rollback Enacted — Effective July 1, 2026
Signed by Gov. Bob Ferguson. Eliminates the sales tax exemption for replacement or refurbishment of existing server equipment as of July 1, 2026. New equipment in qualifying new data centers remains exempt. Prior to this, operators paid zero on the 6.5% state sales tax for server refreshes every 3–5 years. Affects brownfield and upgrade plays materially; new greenfield in eligible counties is unaffected.
Source: E&E News, Apr. 8, 2026 — eenews.net  |  Leyton, Apr. 27, 2026 — leyton.com
Washington UTC — Large Load Policy Docket Active — Decision Expected Late 2026
The Washington Utilities and Transportation Commission launched a multi-month rulemaking after HB 2515 failed, to set policy for large-load connections (>20 MW) to investor-owned utilities (PSE, PacifiCorp). Will issue a policy statement on interconnection cost allocation and ratepayer protections. Does not apply to consumer-owned utilities — Grant PUD, Douglas PUD, Chelan PUD remain outside its jurisdiction entirely. That carve-out is structural, not subject to this docket.
Source: KHQ, Apr. 21, 2026 — khq.com
Executive Order 25-05 — Data Center Workgroup Active — Final Report Expected 2026
Gov. Ferguson's cross-agency workgroup delivered its preliminary report December 1, 2025. Final report expected later this year. Top recommendation: Washington's Climate Commitment Act and Clean Energy Transformation Act (CETA) should explicitly cover fossil or unspecified power used by data centers. The final report will directly frame 2027 legislation. The workgroup also flagged that Washington has no property tax incentives while 37 other states do — a data point that cuts both ways politically.
Source: WA Data Center Workgroup Preliminary Report, Dec. 1, 2025 — dor.wa.gov (PDF)
RCW 82.08.986 — Sales Tax Exemption (Remaining, New Builds) In Force (Partial)
New data center builds in rural counties (under 100 persons/sq mi, or under 225 sq mi) carry uncapped sales tax exemptions on qualifying equipment — no certificate limit. Urban counties (population over 800K) are capped at 6 certificates per year statewide. Requires minimum investment of $200M and 35+ new jobs. Eastern WA counties remain the clearest path to uncapped exemptions. Note: the Seattle and Spokane moratoria do not affect tax eligibility, but they do block permitting within city limits, making the exemption moot for urban sites during the moratorium period.
Source: WA Dept. of Revenue — dor.wa.gov
SEPA — State Environmental Policy Act Baseline Requirement
Every WA project requires SEPA environmental review (WAC 197-11). Lead agency is typically the local jurisdiction. New water withdrawals require a separate Ecology permit. Air permits required for backup diesel generators — regulated by local clean air agencies or Ecology depending on county. The Spokane moratorium hearing on July 22 may produce a SEPA-linked permitting framework specific to data centers for Spokane County. For projects near tribal lands, government-to-government consultation required and can significantly extend timelines.
Source: WA Dept. of Ecology — ecology.wa.gov
→ Updated Acquisition Implication for WA

Western Washington is now operationally off the table for hyperscaler land plays for at least the next 12 months. Seattle and Spokane moratoria are binding. Skagit County adds to the list. The urban 6-certificate cap was already a constraint; the moratoria layer on top of it to make permitting impossible regardless of tax eligibility.

Eastern Washington — Grant, Douglas, Kittitas counties — remains the defensible position. The consumer-owned utility carve-out from the UTC docket is structural, the tax exemptions are uncapped, and no moratorium is in place in those counties. However, the 500 MW Avista situation in Spokane County shows how fast community opposition can materialize even in eastern WA. Underwrite eastern WA assuming the HB 2515 framework passes in 2027, meaning any site over 20 MW should model mandatory clean energy procurement from day one.

Oregon

Legislative & Regulatory Landscape

The Core Story

Oregon's story has moved significantly since the last update. The POWER Act is fully live. But two major new developments change the tax picture materially: Oregon's enterprise zone property tax exemption — the state's signature incentive — is now under a moratorium for new data centers effective June 5, 2026, and advocacy groups have sued Hillsboro and Washington County over exemptions rushed through before the deadline. Meanwhile, Oregon's Data Center Advisory Committee is actively meeting, with a final report due October 2026 that will shape 2027 legislation. The property tax advantage that made Oregon compelling is no longer available to new projects.

HB 4084 — Enterprise Zone Moratorium for Data Centers New In Force — Effective June 5, 2026
This is the biggest change since the last update. The 2026 Oregon Legislature passed HB 4084, which as enacted bars new data centers from accessing the state's Enterprise Zone property tax exemption program. The moratorium remains in effect until 90 days after the 2027 Oregon legislative session adjourns — potentially extending into fall 2027 or later. Prior to this, local governments could grant unlimited, no-cap property tax exemptions to data centers with no job creation requirement — a structural advantage worth over $450M/year statewide.

What's still available: data centers approved before June 5 keep their exemptions. Data centers may still access the Strategic Investment Program, a separate state property tax incentive not covered by the moratorium. The rush to beat the June 5 deadline generated 17 applications in Hillsboro in a matter of weeks — which is now the subject of litigation (see below).
Source: Davis Wright Tremaine, Jun. 2026 — dwt.com  |  City of Hillsboro — hillsboro-oregon.gov  |  National Law Review, May 29, 2026 — natlawreview.com
Hillsboro / Washington County — Lawsuit Over Rushed Tax Breaks New Active — Filed Jun 23, 2026
A coalition including 1000 Friends of Oregon, Oregon Education Association, Tax Fairness Oregon, Tualatin Riverkeepers, and a sitting Hillsboro city councilor filed suit in Washington County Circuit Court seeking to overturn enterprise zone tax exemptions approved for projects tied to NVIDIA, CoreWeave, Adobe, Dropbox, QTS, and Flexential. The city approved 17 applications in the weeks before the June 5 moratorium — some for companies stacking successive 5-year exemption applications to lock in tax-free status for decades. The lawsuit alleges: failure to follow required procedures, insufficient evidence of economic benefit, and that some agreements exceed the enterprise zone's legal lifespan. Hillsboro disputes the claims. Outcome is uncertain but creates title/approval risk for any buyer of affected sites.
Source: KATU News, Jun. 25, 2026 — katu.com  |  Data Centers Daily Notes, Jun. 23, 2026 — strisker.com
Oregon Data Center Advisory Committee (DCAC) New Active — Draft Report Jul 31, Final Oct 2026
Formed by Gov. Kotek in January 2026 alongside the HB 4084 moratorium, the DCAC has completed 5 of its sessions (most recently June 26, 2026, focused on energy affordability, revenue, and incentives). The committee's charge: "identify ways to better balance economic opportunities with clean energy goals and affordable, reliable electricity." Draft report due July 31, 2026. Final report and policy recommendations in October 2026. This will directly shape 2027 Oregon legislation. The committee's work creates a window to engage and influence policy outcomes — but that window is narrow. Industry engagement before the July 31 draft matters.
Source: Oregon Governor's Office, DCAC Agenda Jun. 26, 2026 — oregon.gov (PDF)  |  Davis Wright Tremaine — dwt.com
HB 3546 — POWER Act (Protecting Oregonians With Energy Responsibility) Enacted June 2025 — Fully Live
Creates a standalone large-load rate class for data centers at investor-owned utilities (PGE, Pacific Power, Idaho Power). Applies to facilities using >20 MW. PGE's new rate structure (Schedule 96) took effect June 10, 2026. Key provisions: data centers pay 100% of distribution infrastructure costs; must comply with HB 2021 emissions obligations; pay 1¢/kWh surcharge for facilities over 100 MW into a low-income efficiency fund; must pay exit fees if they abandon a project mid-construction; infrastructure cost obligations are indefinite (PUC rejected a 10-year cap). Rehearing requests due July 6 — possible further modifications. Pacific Power's proceeding expected to conclude November 2026. Does not apply to consumer-owned utilities.
Source: Utility Dive, May 28, 2026 — utilitydive.com  |  OPB, May 12, 2026 — opb.org
HB 2021 — Clean Electricity Mandate Existing Law — Binding for Data Centers
Oregon requires 50% renewable electricity by 2040 and 100% clean by 2040 for large utilities. The PUC's POWER Act order explicitly requires data centers in PGE territory not to undermine this obligation. Clean energy compliance is not optional — it is now embedded in the interconnection approval process under Schedule 96.
Source: US Data Map — usdatamap.com
Goal 14 — Urban Growth Boundary Requirements Ongoing Constraint
Oregon's statewide land use planning law requires data centers to be sited within urban growth boundaries or designated industrial zones. Hillsboro/Washington County is heavily built out — 21 existing or in-progress data center sites covering ~470 acres, representing 8% of the North Hillsboro Industrial Area. Five more planned sites would add ~177 acres but those are already submitted. The practical effect: the Hillsboro pipeline is mostly spoken for. Eastern Oregon (Morrow, Umatilla, Wasco counties) has available industrial-zoned land with existing power infrastructure and no similar land constraint.
Source: City of Hillsboro, Jun. 2026 — hillsboro-oregon.gov
Strategic Investment Program — Property Tax Incentive (Still Available) In Force — Not Covered by Moratorium
The HB 4084 moratorium covers the Enterprise Zone program only. Oregon's Strategic Investment Program is a separate state property tax incentive that remains available to data centers. The SIP typically applies to very large capital investments and involves negotiated agreements with local governments. This is not a simple plug-in replacement for the enterprise zone exemption — it requires more negotiation, higher investment thresholds, and community benefit agreements — but it preserves some property tax relief pathway for major projects. Details on current SIP eligibility and thresholds should be confirmed with Oregon Business Oregon (the state economic development agency).
Source: Davis Wright Tremaine, Jun. 2026 — dwt.com
DEQ Tier 4 Streamlined Air Permit Available Program
Oregon DEQ's streamlined permitting pathway for data centers using EPA Tier 4 certified backup diesel generators. As of 2025, Oregon had 40+ data centers with 6,000 MW of backup generating capacity — double statewide residential usage. Tier 4 commitment enables faster air permitting and is increasingly a hyperscaler specification requirement. Pre-establishing Tier 4 compatibility in site analysis is a selling point for buyers.
Source: Oregon DEQ — oregon.gov/deq
→ Updated Acquisition Implication for OR

The property tax picture in Oregon changed materially in June 2026. The enterprise zone exemption — previously unlimited and no-cap — is now unavailable for new data centers until at least fall 2027. The Strategic Investment Program remains available but is not a simple substitute. Oregon's no-sales-tax advantage remains intact, and hydropower rates remain competitive, but the combination of POWER Act rate increases and the enterprise zone moratorium means the cost model for new Oregon projects looks meaningfully different than it did six months ago.

The Hillsboro lawsuit adds title risk for any site whose recent tax exemptions were part of the rushed approval wave. Eastern Oregon (Morrow, Umatilla, Wasco counties) is the cleaner play: industrial zoning exists, consumer-owned utilities sidestep the POWER Act, and the enterprise zone moratorium still applies but eastern Oregon's lower land costs and existing BPA infrastructure may compensate. The DCAC draft report on July 31 is the next critical date — get in front of that process if possible.

Federal

Federal Overlay

FERC — Six Large Load Interconnection Orders New Issued June 18, 2026
FERC unanimously approved six orders on June 18 requiring all major U.S. grid operators to fast-track AI data center grid connections within 30- and 60-day federal deadlines, while explicitly barring utilities from shifting interconnection costs to existing ratepayers. Covers PJM, MISO, SPP, CAISO, and others. The Pacific Northwest falls under BPA/WECC jurisdiction, so direct applicability varies — but the federal direction of travel is unambiguous. These orders do not override state land use, zoning, or retail utility rate regulations. FERC Chair Laura Swett framed this as the federal government treating AI power demand as a "national infrastructure crisis."
Source: TechTimes, Jun. 20, 2026 — techtimes.com
Trump Executive Orders + "Ratepayer Protection Pledge" Federal Policy
The Trump administration streamlined federal environmental review for facilities over 100 MW or $500M investment. Per MultiState (May 2026), these orders do not override state authority on land use, zoning, or utility regulations. States with independent air quality programs have maintained their own review timelines regardless. The voluntary Ratepayer Protection Pledge does not bind WA or OR regulators.
Source: MultiState, May 1, 2026 — multistate.us
→ Bottom Line on Federal vs. State

The FERC orders are meaningful for transmission queue timing but do not touch land use, zoning, moratorium decisions, or state utility rate structures. Washington and Oregon retain full authority over the variables that actually determine site feasibility. Federal tailwind is real at the transmission level — it does not shorten Spokane's moratorium or reopen Oregon's enterprise zone exemption.
